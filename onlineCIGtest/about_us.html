About Us

This website is a working model of the research we are conducting at Texas Tech University as part of our exploration of declarative programming and its applications for increasing the accessibility of Clinical Practice Guidelines and potential comorbidities. It is developed to accurately intake patient information and output a streamlined list of medications that complies with the diseases’ clinical practice guidelines.

It is estimated that over 98,000 preventable deaths occur every year due to medical errors. With such high numbers, it is becoming ever more important to find methods to reduce mortality and aid doctors in eliminating errors. One of the established approaches is the creation of evidence based clinical practice guidelines to help standardize and improve the quality of medical care patients receive. However, the information heavy nature and inconvenient format of clinical practice guidelines are major obstacles in the eventual goal of widespread usability. These guidelines are often in the form of a lengthy article and reading them all is an impossibility for doctors with already numerous time constraints. For these reasons, clinical practice guidelines cannot fulfill its full potential to aid in mortality and mistake reduction. In addition to the accessibility problems, the biggest challenge doctors face is that these clinical practice guidelines present lots of redundancies and interactions when applied to patients with multiple diseases. Because they are not adapted for patients with comorbidities, it becomes very difficult for physicians to follow these guidelines. Having an automated system would result in an increase in accessibility and the production of a tool doctors can utilize to better apply clinical practice guidelines for comorbidities. Thus, our research focuses on a solution for this problem by exploring the potential declarative programming has in maximizing both the accessibility and automation of clinical practice guidelines. Declarative languages distinguish themselves from other languages as prime candidates for this purpose because of the ease with which English sentences and ideas can be translated into functional code. This ability of declarative programming to facilitate direct translation makes it an excellent tool in the creation of a complex program full of complicated medical jargon and interactions. In addition, we intended to exploit the non-monotonicity of declarative programming to add critical flexibility to our sample program. In our research, the specific declarative language we have chosen to explore and test is answer set prolog. Through this project, we embark on an exploration of the role answer set programming could play in promoting increased and more thorough usage of clinical practice guidelines.
